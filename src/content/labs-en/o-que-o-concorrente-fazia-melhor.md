A competitor started taking my clients, and I had a certainty on the tip of my tongue: "their pizza is better". Every owner has that kind of certainty. The problem is that almost nobody stops to check whether it's true.

I wasn't going to spend energy fixing the wrong thing. Before touching anything in the operation, I needed to know **exactly** what the customer was seeing that was different.

## Hunches don't decide. Evidence does.

Instead of opinion, I gathered proof. Dozens of real photos of my product and theirs, the way it arrives at the customer's door. Both storefronts on iFood, side by side. The menus. What the customers themselves post.

It's not "I think so". It's product against product, same flavor against same flavor, looking at what the customer's eye looks at when choosing.

## The myths falling one by one

The funny part was watching my certainties die in the crossing:

- **"Their crust is better."** It wasn't. Mine tied, sometimes won.
- **"Their iFood photo is better."** A tie. Both had a good storefront.
- **"They're cheaper."** False. They charged more.

Three reasons I'd give off the top of my head, all three wrong. If I'd acted on instinct, I'd have changed price or recipe, spent energy, and moved the needle not one millimeter.

## What it actually was

The difference was in a place I wasn't even looking: **the finish and consistency of what arrives at the customer's door.** The part the eye reads first, before any bite.

> It wasn't capability. It was execution standard.

And that's the best possible news, because execution standard is fixable. I didn't need a new kitchen or a new recipe. I needed a control point where there was none. The proof is in the items where I already took care: on those, I tied or won. The problem was never **being able** to do it. It was doing it the same, every time.
