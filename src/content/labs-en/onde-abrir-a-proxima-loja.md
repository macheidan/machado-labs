Opening a new store is the most expensive decision an owner makes, and it's money that doesn't come back if you get the location wrong. Even so, almost everyone picks where to open on feeling: a nice spot they drove past, a rent that came up cheap, a neighborhood that "seems like it sells". I wasn't going to bet that on a hunch.

What almost nobody notices is that **the map for the decision already exists, for free**. It's iFood.

## The whole city, neighborhood by neighborhood

The marketplace sees the food demand of the entire city, month by month, by area. Where people order a lot, where they order little, how much they spend in each place. It's the market research I'd pay a consultancy good money for, and it was right there, up to date, waiting for someone to read it.

I crossed two things: **how many people order pizza in each neighborhood** and **how much my brand already shows up there**. The first is the size of the plate. The second is how much of it I'm already eating.

## Where the gap was

The target jumped out of that crossing: an area with a lot of hunger and almost no presence of mine. Plenty of demand, little Lov. And with a care that feeling ignores: **far enough from my current stores that I wouldn't steal from myself.** A new store that cannibalizes the old one isn't growth, it's moving money from one pocket to another.

That's what data solves and instinct doesn't: it shows the real distance between where I already serve and where I haven't reached yet.

## The part that almost made me quit

Here's what struck me most, and what most people never get to see.

The same map showed a neighborhood where I **already had an operation** and still barely showed up, in a huge market. In other words: the data almost convinced me **not** to open a store.

> Sometimes the next growth isn't one more store. It's converting the one you already have better.

Before spending on a new location, there's money sitting idle in the operation I already pay for every month. A guess would never have told me that. The data told me on the first look.

***

Swapping the expansion decision from feeling to the data the company already generates is exactly the kind of project I help companies build. It's the same principle as [putting the monthly close on autopilot](/en/labs/dre-no-automatico/): decide with a number, not with a feeling.

**Stack:** iFood demand data by neighborhood, over a window of a few months, crossed with the stores' internal sales, turned into a heat map by area and a brand-presence index for each zone, to separate where there's a big market and little of my brand from where I'd only cannibalize what I already have.
